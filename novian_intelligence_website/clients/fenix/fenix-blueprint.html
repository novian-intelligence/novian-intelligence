01 Executive Summary

The business runs. The business is at risk. Both are true.

Fenix Post Tension is a profitable, trusted, 20-year Central Texas supplier moving roughly four hundred foundations a month at peak. Customers stay for two decades. The quality is real. The margins are healthy.

And yet the entire operational intelligence of the company — which builder gets which pricing, what a plan takes to quote, when to reorder cable, which jobs are priority, how a new hire learns the trade — lives in three people's heads and one wobbly binder. The estimating genius is your dad's. The inventory discipline is Larry's. The coordination choreography is Carlos's. None of it is written down. None of it is queryable. None of it is transferable.

The risk isn't today. The risk is the day one of those brains walks out the door — retirement, illness, a better offer — and the business discovers it cannot function without them. Consolidators like QXO are already buying up post-tensioning shops and undercutting on price. Competing on relationships alone is a closing window.

The Strategic Bet

Don't rebuild Fenix. Build a shadow twin of Fenix — a second operating system that runs in parallel, captures everything the humans do, learns the patterns, and slowly becomes capable of doing more of the work itself. Dad keeps doing dad. The company gets modern underneath him.

02 The Landscape

Where the friction lives.

What follows is what we heard in a ninety-minute conversation — organized into six clusters, each rated for risk severity and paired with the business impact of leaving it as-is.

This isn't a gotcha list. These are the places where a company built on relationships runs face-first into the limits of memory and paper.

01
Plans Intake & Estimating
Critical
What We Heard
  • Builder plans arrive as PDFs scattered across individual Gmail inboxes — and Dad's Yahoo.
  • No unified intake address. If a plan lands in the wrong person's inbox, it sits there.
  • Dad personally reads every plan and produces every takeoff, by eye and experience.
  • Typical quote turnaround is ~1 week. Dad doesn't know the industry average. Nobody does.
  • No queue. No priority view. No visibility into which quotes are in flight.
  • The pricing matrix lives partially in Excel and partially in Dad's twenty-year memory.
"There's not really a queue that you can look at and just be like, okay, this one is the one that's most important right now."
Business Impact
  • Single point of failure: if Dad is out, quoting stops entirely.
  • Turnaround likely 2–3x slower than industry leaders — measurable revenue left on the table as builders go elsewhere for urgent jobs.
  • Impossible to benchmark or improve a process that isn't instrumented.
  • The company's most valuable IP — how Dad prices — walks out the door when he does.
  • Near-zero leverage: Dad's output is capped at Dad's personal capacity.
02
Scheduling & Job Operations
Critical
What We Heard
  • Scheduling lives in one Google Sheet maintained by Carlos and a few others.
  • Drivers, field crews, warehouse, and office coordinate via individual texts and phone calls — no shared visibility.
  • Field contractors (not employees) call in when jobs are done; someone in the office manually notes it.
  • ~10–15% of jobs have change orders — all handled verbally, rippled to production by voice.
  • Builders call Fenix to ask where their job is in the queue, because they have no other way to find out.
"If that spreadsheet had its own section... its own login for a fucking builder to see where in the process their house is — because sometimes they'll call to just know that."
Business Impact
  • Office time absorbed by status-check phone calls that could be self-serve.
  • No way to measure cycle time, delivery performance, or on-time rate — you're flying blind on operational KPIs.
  • Change orders routed verbally invite miscommunication and waste.
  • Jason has no visibility when he's not physically in the office, which means he has to be in the office.
  • Zero competitive marketing story (e.g. "fastest turnaround in Central Texas") because no data backs it.
03
Inventory & Supply Chain
Critical
What We Heard
  • Larry (warehouse manager, late 60s) tracks all material in a physical binder. Pen and paper.
  • Reorder decisions are based on Larry's mental model of "job trends" — no formula, no data.
  • No real-time answer to "do we have the cable for this week's jobs?"
  • Material arrives via multiple suppliers (direct-from-China via brother, U.S. middlemen) with no unified view.
  • Mid-job shortages happen; they're resolved reactively by phone.
"If we have a screw to leave the warehouse, how do we know?"
Business Impact
  • Capital tied up in over-ordering as a hedge against uncertainty.
  • Stockouts cause job delays, which damage builder relationships.
  • When Larry retires, the business loses institutional memory of every supplier, every spec, every price point.
  • No ability to attribute material cost accurately to specific jobs — margin analysis is approximation.
  • No leverage for supplier negotiation — nobody can see consumption patterns in aggregate.
04
Customer Experience & Relationships
High
What We Heard
  • No CRM. Client contact info is scattered across individual Gmail inboxes and one shared spreadsheet.
  • No client portal. Builders have no way to self-serve job status, documents, or invoice history.
  • Google reviews are positive but sparse — no systematic incentive to capture them.
  • 80% of revenue is repeat business, but there's no relationship management layer, just personal habits.
  • Christmas gift baskets are the retention strategy. (Which is lovely, but unscalable.)
Business Impact
  • Growth is bottlenecked by founder-led relationships that don't transfer.
  • Builder churn risk is invisible until it's too late — no health-score on any account.
  • Review scarcity hurts both SEO and social proof at exactly the moment consolidators are buying up competitors.
  • Missed cross-sell / upsell opportunities — no tracking of which customers use which products.
05
Knowledge & Documentation
Critical
What We Heard
  • No employee handbook. No SOPs. The OSHA manual is "under a wobbly table somewhere."
  • Past jobs are stored in binders — you cannot query "what did we do for this builder in 2021?"
  • New hires learn by shadowing the warehouse manager. Tribal knowledge, start to finish.
  • Custom pricing for long-term clients lives only in Dad's head and email history.
  • Jason has started drafting SOPs with ChatGPT during slow months but hasn't had time to push further.
Business Impact
  • The company is not sellable at fair value without documented processes.
  • Succession is a leap of faith, not a transfer of assets.
  • Onboarding new hires takes longer than it should and produces inconsistent results.
  • Every time a long-tenured employee leaves, the company loses institutional knowledge permanently.
06
Tech Foundation & Web Presence
High
What We Heard
  • Mixed email environment: most of the team on Gmail, Dad on Yahoo. No shared identity domain.
  • Website is on Wix. SEO has slipped off the first page of Google.
  • Blog exists but has been abandoned.
  • No internal messaging (no Slack, no Teams). Communication is texts and calls.
  • No paid ads, no lead tracking, no website analytics review.
  • Networking, camera systems, and hardware foundation are actually solid — Jason has already run Unifi throughout.
Business Impact
  • Missing shared identity (@fenixposttension.com) makes everything downstream — CRM, document sharing, AI automation — harder to deploy cleanly.
  • Losing SEO position to competitors is losing inbound pipeline for the 20% of net-new revenue.
  • Zero internal comms tooling means coordination friction scales linearly with headcount — it's already hurting a 25-person company.
  • You can't instrument what you can't route through a common system.
03 Strategy

Five principles that govern every decision in this plan.

Before any tool selection, any phase, any investment — these five rules shape the approach. When in doubt, come back here.

01
Parallel, never replace.
Nothing we build removes a tool, inbox, or workflow that Dad, Larry, or Carlos currently depends on. New systems run in addition to old ones until value is self-evident. Day one of every new tool: the old way still works exactly as before.
02
Visibility before automation.
You cannot automate a process you cannot see. The first act of every workstream is to make the current (manual) process measurable and observable. Only then do we decide what to automate and what to preserve as human judgment.
03
Capture tribal knowledge as insurance.
Treat Dad's estimating intuition, Larry's supplier playbook, and Carlos's scheduling dance as irreplaceable business assets. Document them as we go — with their consent — into a structured knowledge base that outlives any one person.
04
Adopt before building.
For any need, we try SaaS first. Custom-built only where SaaS fails — usually because (a) no vendor serves the post-tensioning niche, or (b) integration across systems demands glue code we have to write ourselves. AI agents are the glue layer.
05
Every phase must show Dad-proof ROI.
Each phase is designed to produce one undeniable, visible-in-a-spreadsheet business win within 30–60 days. Not theoretical value. Actual dollars, hours, or new leads. That is how a 60-year-old founder changes his mind — evidence, not evangelism.
06
Jason's POC, Jason's leverage.
Because you (Jason) are self-funding the POC, the entire early-phase plan must be achievable on a personal budget — under a few hundred dollars a month in tools. We prove the model, then the business buys in. No asking Dad to approve anything until there's a working demo to point at.

06 The Dad Plan

The single biggest risk factor is Dad.
The single biggest asset is also Dad.

Every transformation of a family business fails or succeeds on this one question: what do you do with the founder who built the thing, knows everything, and has every reason to resist change? The answer is not to fight him. The answer is to build around him, with him, in service of him.

"My dad is just — to him, everything is like fucking voodoo. He's never liked anything I've suggested or done. So I just have to do it."
— Jason, during the interview
Move 01 / Parallel Tracks

Dad's workflow never changes — not for a single day.

Every new system runs alongside the one Dad uses, never as a replacement. He continues to receive plans in his Yahoo-migrated Gmail. He continues to read them the way he always has. He continues to produce takeoffs in Excel. The new system silently captures his inputs and outputs — builds a parallel structured record — and over time learns his pricing patterns. Dad does not need to approve, adopt, or even notice. When he finally does notice, it will be because Jason shows him a dashboard with his own numbers on it and says, "look what your expertise built."

Move 02 / Legacy Framing

Position everything as protecting Dad's legacy, not replacing it.

Dad has twenty years of pricing intuition that no software, no agency, no successor can match. That intuition is the most valuable asset in the company. It's also dangerously stored in exactly one brain. The modernization is not about making Dad obsolete — it's about making sure his life's work survives him. Every interview, every SOP, every documented process is a tribute to how much of what he does genuinely cannot be replaced. Lead with that. He'll listen differently.

Move 03 / Evidence, Not Evangelism

Show him the before-and-after, measured in dollars or hours.

Don't pitch Dad on agents, or AI, or automation, or platforms. Pitch him on: "we used to spend X hours a week on status calls from Lennar; now it's Y because they self-serve." Or: "quote turnaround used to be a week; now it's two days — and Lennar just called me asking if we could handle their Houston volume too." Dad did not build this company on theory. He built it on results. Bring him results.

Move 04 / The Mentor Path

Honor his stated goal of mentoring a successor.

He's said he wants to mentor someone into the business. Structure the knowledge-capture interviews as that mentorship — just with an AI teammate quietly documenting every session. Dad teaches. Jason listens. Claude transcribes, structures, and stores. Dad gets to do the thing he wants (pass wisdom down); the business gets the permanent asset it needs (captured institutional knowledge). Nobody has to call it "documentation."
