01 Track One · Side A

The Story So Far

Before we change anything, let's be clear-eyed about what's already true. Most of this playbook is amplification, not replacement.

Jon — you spent twelve years as a full-time DJ in New York City and Austin. That's not a footnote on your résumé. That's the entire foundation of why you're going to be very good at this job.

What does a working DJ actually do? You read a room. You watch how a body of strangers responds to a frequency, a tempo, a transition. You build trust in seven seconds with people who will never know your name. You hold a thousand variables in your head at once — the next track, the energy curve, the bride's mom on the dance floor, the catering manager wanting to switch to dinner music, the bar running low. You make people feel something they came specifically to feel, and you bill them for it.

That's the same job as outbound sales. The deck is different, the venue is HubSpot, the dance floor is LinkedIn, and the booth fee is paid out monthly instead of nightly. But the muscle — reading rooms, building trust fast, holding a complicated pipeline of variables in your head, being honest about where the energy actually is — that muscle is the most valuable thing in the entire AV industry, and you've been training it since you were in your twenties.

What you already do well

Three months in, you've already built things most reps don't build in a year. You have 379 target accounts, 89 win-backs, 172 past DJ orgs mapped, 130 event planners, 40 venues, 56 marketing-agency contacts, 50 power-partner industry folks, and a list of 240 conferences coming to Austin. You joined MPI two weeks ago and now have a national directory. You write 25 different email templates segmented by vertical. You go through Gmail with the HubSpot plugin. You record your calls. You leave voicemails. You log everything — you said you "love your CRM hygiene" and that's not a small thing.

You estimate a 10% cold-email response rate. Industry average is two to three. You're getting 40–50% LinkedIn connection acceptance in your space because you're targeting carefully. You closed your first deal (Olaplex), and you turned a no-promise meeting into three RFPs in the pipeline by being good in the room. You've already convinced your CEO with a piece-to-camera LinkedIn post that he literally praised you for, even though you said the recording made you self-conscious. You're three months in and there's a five-million-dollar inventory of LED walls, robotic cameras, and motion-sensor systems sitting in a studio waiting for the rooms you're going to fill with them.

The instinct, the output, the professionalism — all real, all already happening. You named those as your strengths and they are. None of what comes next replaces them.

My biggest strengths are my instincts. I know how to feel. I'm very intuitive with my prospects. How to, when to pull back, when to connect with them.

— Jon, in his own words

What this playbook is, and is not

This is not a "let AI do your job" document. You'd be a fool to want that and we'd be fools to suggest it. The thing that closes deals at $30k to $500k is human trust between two adults who can read each other across a table. AI can't replace that and shouldn't try.

This is a manual for taking everything that currently lives in your notebook, your head, your spreadsheets, and your gut — and giving it scaffolding, leverage, and a memory. So you can spend more of your day in the rooms (literal and digital) where you do your best work, and less of it doing the parts of the job that drain you and that a machine handles in three seconds.

It's also — and you said this yourself in the closing minutes of the interview — about getting your fifteen-minute morning meditation back. About being present at dinner with your kids by 5pm. About hitting your $200k income number without burning the candle at both ends. The point of automation in 2026 is not to work more; it's to make the work you already do count for more, so you can close the laptop earlier and pour into the people you actually live with.

Twelve years of reading rooms is a hell of a foundation. Let's build the rest of the house on top of it.

Where the leaks are

The trouble isn't your effort. The trouble is that you're a one-person sales department who is also a one-person research department, content department, analytics department, follow-up department, and rev-ops department. There's only one Jon, and Jon needs to sleep and parent his kids. Here's where the floor is wet:

The visibility leak

You have 25 templates and you don't know which ones are actually working. You can see if a single email was opened only when you click into a contact — you don't get a real-time signal when a hot prospect re-opens your message six times in an afternoon. You said it yourself: "I'd love some sort of way to get a prompt or to know if I'm getting that to motivate me." Without that feedback loop, you're flying without instruments.

The memory leak

Every phone call you make through HubSpot is recorded and transcribed. Almost none of those transcripts ever get read again. Action items live in your head. Notes about a prospect get buried in the contact's activity feed within a week. You sometimes fall back on a paper notebook because the digital workflow doesn't surface the past clearly enough. That's not a Jon problem — it's a tooling-not-doing-its-job problem.

The research leak

You spend real time prospecting one company at a time — figuring out which Dell person owns events, scrolling LinkedIn for the right communications director, screen-shotting conferences out of a paper magazine because the data isn't in a CSV anywhere. This is exactly the work that AI does instantly, accurately, and at scale — and it's currently the work eating your daylight.

The nurture leak

You said you'd love to send a prospect "hey, I thought of you, here's an article" — the kind of touch that turns a cold lead into a warm one over six months. But you're not doing it yet, because there's no content shelf to pull from and no system to remind you who'd care about what. So follow-ups default to "just checking in," which is the weakest move in the book.

The collateral leak

The case studies on the website are dated. The Canva decks aren't being refreshed because your one marketing person is stretched thin. You're being told to make your own one-sheeters. So the moment a prospect says "send me something to share with my team," you're either delaying or sending dated material into a high-stakes buying conversation. That's a real revenue leak we can plug fast.

The technical-depth leak

You named this honestly: you're not an AV technician, and sometimes you struggle to translate value props into the customer's specific business case mid-call. Right now, there's no co-pilot in the room with you. There can be one by next month.

Reframe

"I'm pretty much inventing the wheel myself."

That's not a complaint. That's a statement of fact you made about a job your CEO partially scoped using ChatGPT. You're three months in and the foundation underneath you is being built as you walk on it.

Not because the work gets easier. Because you'll see the work compounding instead of feeling like you threw another day into the wind.

How to think about the sequencing

Don't do all of Tier 01 in one weekend. The temptation will be to set up six tools at once, get overwhelmed, and revert to the old workflow inside two weeks. Pick two from Tier 01 in week one. Add two more in week two. Get those into your muscle memory before stacking the next layer.

Tier 02 is where the business case to your CEO lives. When you ask for $300/month in tooling, you'll have receipts from Tier 01 to show what already worked — and you'll be asking for the next investment that turns proven habit into pipeline outcomes. Track 10 has the actual one-pager you'd hand him.

Tier 03 is the Andrei project. This is the part where your friend who builds custom Claude agents on weekends rolls up his sleeves. You're not setting that up alone. You're scoping what you want it to do for you, and then it gets built around your real workflow rather than the other way around. That's why this isn't on you to figure out — it's a partnership build.

05 Track Five · Side B

The MPI WEC Play

3 days, ~2,000 attendees, San Antonio in June. The single highest-leverage event on your calendar this year. Here's how to make it count.

You joined MPI specifically because of WEC. You said it yourself: "part of my role is to develop national agency relationships." WEC is where 2,000 of those people will be in one room, in your home state, for three days. Walking in cold and being friendly is the default. Walking in with a system is the multiplier.

The math of why this matters

If you have 50 meaningful conversations across three days — which is realistic for someone with your DJ-honed rapport skills — and 15% of those turn into a real follow-up dialog post-conference, you're walking out with seven to eight new active relationships with national event planners and association leaders. At a conservative 10% close rate over 12 months on those, even at your $30k average event size, that's $24k of revenue from one conference. At your sweet spot of $100k+ events with national accounts, it's a quarter of your annual quota in one weekend.

That's only true if you have the systems in place to actually convert handshakes into pipeline. Below is the full playbook — pre, during, and post.

Pre-Conference · April–May (now → 6 weeks out)

  • Pull the MPI national directory and filter to your ICP. Corporate event planners and association event leads only. Geography weighted toward Texas + states where ATXES has done events. Aim for a working list of 200–300 names.
  • Cross-reference the WEC attendee list. WEC publishes attendee info on the event site or in the app pre-event. Scrape it (Andrei can help with this) and intersect with your filtered MPI list. Now you have your pre-targeted attendees — probably 60–100 names.
  • Score them. Quick three-tier ranking: (A) priority — book a meeting in advance, (B) high-interest — intentional bump-into, (C) net-cast — happy if it happens. Aim for 20 in tier A, 40 in B, the rest in C.
  • Pre-conference outreach. 4–5 weeks out, send a personalized note to each tier-A contact: "I'll be at WEC, here's why I'd love 15 minutes — coffee on me Tuesday morning?" Realistic conversion: 30–50% will accept a meeting.
  • Build the conference toolkit. A 30-second elevator pitch tested out loud. A QR-coded business card linking to a personalized landing page (one-click build with Cloud + Carrd, ~$20). A clean one-sheeter version of the Roadmap to Success that fits in a pocket.
// Six weeks before WEC, run this: "Here's the WEC attendee list [paste/attach] and my MPI member filters: corporate event planners, association event leads, anyone with 'events', 'meetings', 'conferences' in title. For each match, rank A/B/C based on: seniority, employer size, geographic relevance to Texas, and any signals of upcoming events. Output as a table I can import into HubSpot with a column for 'pre-conference outreach status'."

During Conference · The 3 Days

  • Run your tier-A meetings cleanly. 15–20 minutes each, you've prepped a one-page brief on each person the night before (Claude generates these from their LinkedIn + company news). You walk in already knowing what matters to them.
  • Use a digital business-card scanner from minute one. CamCard or HiHello, free tier — every card you collect becomes a HubSpot contact within 30 seconds. Don't end the day with 80 cards in your pocket and no plan.
  • End-of-day voice-memo dump. Walk back to your hotel. Open the voice recorder. Talk for 10 minutes about every meaningful person you met today — what they care about, what you talked about, what you promised to follow up on. That voice memo gets transcribed by Claude overnight and turned into individual HubSpot notes per contact, ready for morning.
  • LinkedIn-add the same day. Personal note: "Great meeting at WEC — loved hearing about [thing they specifically mentioned]. Following up properly next week." 90% acceptance rate when sent the same day. Drops to 40% if you wait two weeks.
// Each evening at WEC, after your voice memo: "Here's a 10-minute voice transcript of every person I met today at WEC [paste]. For each person, generate a HubSpot-ready note: name, company, title, what they care about, what we discussed, what I promised to send or do, and a temperature read (cold/warm/hot). Format as a list I can copy-paste into HubSpot one at a time."

Post-Conference · June–July

  • 48-hour rule. Every single tier-A and tier-B contact gets a personalized email within 48 hours of leaving San Antonio. Specific reference to your conversation. One concrete next step (call, intro, sample of work). Not generic.
  • One-week deep follow-up. For the warmest 10–15, send something tangible: a relevant case study, an intro to a peer, a calendar link for a 30-minute virtual coffee. This is where most reps stop and where you keep going.
  • Pipeline review with your CEO. A clean one-pager: 50+ new contacts, X tier-A meetings booked, Y near-term opportunities, Z long-cycle relationships. He'll see the system. He'll see the ROI. He'll be ready to fund the next round of tooling.
  • Monthly nurture loop. Everyone you met goes into a monthly nurture cadence with content that matches their world. The conference becomes the start of a 12-month relationship campaign, not a single weekend's bag of cards.

Total cost to run this play above your current stack: roughly $50—business card scanner premium tier + a Carrd landing page + a few extra Claude credits. The conference registration and travel are already happening. Everything described above is a workflow upgrade on the trip you've already committed to.

There's going to be a lot of card exchanging and pleasantries that may not go anywhere.

— Jon, on what WEC USUALLY looks like for everyone else

That's the version of WEC where a tired sales rep ends Tuesday with 80 business cards and no plan, sends a generic blast on Friday, and gets two replies. The version of WEC above ends with eight booked follow-ups, fifty enriched HubSpot contacts, twelve real conversations to continue, and a one-page pipeline read for your CEO that justifies whatever comes next. Same conference. Same Jon. Different system underneath.

06 Track Six · Side B

The Encore
Counter-Strategy

Every hero needs a villain. Yours is a $1B+ giant with kickback contracts at most major hotels in your territory. Here's how a boutique outfit beats them anyway.

You spoke about Encore with more energy than almost any other topic in two hours. That's a signal. The path to your $200k year runs through finding the rooms Encore can't lock you out of — and being so much better in those rooms that the prospects start asking for you by name.

The honest competitive picture

You named it cleanly: Encore wins by paying venues to be the exclusive AV provider, then collecting whatever margin the venue allows. That's a real moat. They have multi-year contracts with most of the major hotels and convention centers. When a corporate event planner books the venue, the AV is often already pre-decided in the venue contract before anyone calls you. Freeman, Marquis, and Inspire run a similar playbook.

You also named the cracks in their armor — and these are the cracks the entire counter-strategy is built around:

Crack 01

Customer dissatisfaction

"People don't like working with Encore — they charge a lot or give the bare minimum, not helpful."

Their scale is their weakness. They can't customize. They can't move fast. Their account managers turn over. Their post-event support is uneven. The market knows this.

Crack 02

Off-property events

Their exclusivity is at the venue, not at the client. The client has other events.

A Dell that uses Encore for its President's Club in Vegas still has experiential events, partner summits, exec offsites, and field marketing in venues with no AV exclusive. Win the side door, then the front.

Crack 03

Pre-event experience

Encore competes on the day-of. You compete starting 3 months before.

The Roadmap to Success workflow makes the lead-up to the event Encore-proof. Half the customer satisfaction is built before the doors open — you said that yourself. They can't replicate it.

Four plays you can run starting now

Play 01 · The Listening Tool

This is the one Josh hinted at during the interview, and it's the most important counter-play in the whole document. Set up a monitoring agent that scans Reddit, LinkedIn, G2, Trustpilot, and event-industry forums for any mention of Encore, Freeman, or Marquis paired with words like “disappointed,” “overcharged,” “unresponsive,” “switching providers,” “looking for alternatives.” The agent surfaces the 3–5 best leads per week with the original post and a recommended outreach angle. (Andrei has built this exact system for himself at Hivelocity. It works.)

Play 02 · The Off-Property Wedge

For every corporate target where Encore has the headquarters venue locked, identify their secondary event ecosystem — field marketing, partner summits, customer dinners, regional roadshows, year-end parties. None of those happen at the locked Encore venue. That's your wedge. Win one off-property event with a Dell, do it brilliantly, and you're in the conversation for the President's Club next year.

Play 03 · The Pre-Event Demo

Your Roadmap to Success workflow is your patent. Most prospects have never seen it. Build a 5-minute Loom video walking through it — how the deliverable schedule works, who's responsible for what, the visibility a client gets two months out from their event. Send it as part of every cold outreach to a corporate event planner currently locked into an in-house provider. The pitch isn't "switch to us." The pitch is "this is what working with us actually feels like — you've never had this with Encore."

Play 04 · The Venue Side-Door

Most reps target the venue's sales team. You should also target the venue's F&B managers, banquet managers, and on-property AV operations leads. These people deal with the in-house provider's failures every weekend. They have informal recommendation power when a planner asks privately for a better option. Cultivate three of these per major venue you care about. Lunch them. Send them holiday gifts. They become your inside source.

We are not the cheapest, but we do pay our folks well and have stayed a modern gear. We got our studio — 15 by 40 foot LED wall coming in, robotic cameras, motion sensor stuff.

— Jon, on the actual product story

You called yourself the Lexus to their Toyota. That framing is half right and worth refining. Encore is the corporate fleet vehicle. You're not the Lexus — you're the bespoke instrument the client didn't know they could afford. That's a different sale, told differently. Lead with craftsmanship, partnership, and the experience of working with humans who give a damn. The price objection becomes a value conversation by the second meeting.

One last note on this. Don't badmouth Encore in your sales conversations — ever. The people you're selling to often have to work with Encore because they're locked in. Diplomacy is part of the moat. Let your differentiation do the talking. The villain section is for your internal strategy, not your external script.

Conservative scenario: a 20% lift in qualified pipeline output. At our average deal size of $40k and current close rate, that's roughly $600k of incremental sourced revenue per year. At our gross margins, that's well over 10x the annual investment in tooling. Claude Pro subscription. Activation of HubSpot Sequences and Breeze Studio (already in our stack, currently underused). Free tools: Fathom for call recording, Loom for video outreach, HiHello for business card capture. Goal: prove a measurable lift in weekly output before any larger investment.

How we measure success

Four KPIs, reviewed monthly: (1) qualified meetings booked per week, (2) cold-email response rate, (3) pipeline value added per month, (4) hours per week spent on selling activity vs. admin. Total downside risk on Phase 1 is roughly $66 of subscription cost over 90 days — functionally zero.
