🏢 Company Snapshot

Applied Materials is the world's largest semiconductor equipment company — and one of the most important companies you've never heard of. Founded in 1967 in Mountain View, California, it provides the tools that make chips possible. Not the chips themselves, but the precision equipment that deposits materials atom by atom, etches patterns at nanometer scale, and inspects wafers for defects invisible to the naked eye.


Every modern chip — in your iPhone, your Tesla, your AI data center — was made using Applied Materials equipment. The company is present in virtually every semiconductor fab on earth. As AI drives the most intense chip investment cycle in history, Applied Materials sits at the center of it all, delivering its sixth consecutive year of record revenue in FY2025 with $28.37 billion. CEO Gary Dickerson calls them "well positioned at the highest value technology inflections in the fastest growing areas of the market."

📦 Business Segments

⚙️ Semiconductor Systems (73%)

The core engine. Deposition, etch, ion implantation, metrology, and inspection tools. Used to physically build logic and memory chips. Direct beneficiary of AI-driven fab investment by TSMC, Samsung, Intel, and memory makers.

🔧 Applied Global Services (23%)

Spare parts, maintenance, and proprietary software for fab optimization. Over 90% of contracts are multi-year — a powerful recurring revenue engine that buffers against semiconductor cycle volatility. The "razor blade" to the equipment razor.

🖥️ Display & Adjacent (4%)

Equipment for OLED screens in phones, TVs, and laptops. More cyclical, smaller contribution, but a technology leader in high-resolution display manufacturing. Provides diversification outside pure semiconductor cycles.

The PPACt Strategy — Positioned at Every Major Chip Inflection

Dickerson's PPACt framework (Power, Performance, Area-Cost, Time-to-Market) isn't just a slogan — it's a roadmap that puts AMAT's R&D directly in the path of every chip architect's priority list. As the industry moves from planar transistors to FinFET to GAA to BSPD, Applied Materials has new, proprietary tools at each step. Competitors have to catch up. AMAT gets there first.

🟢 Moat

90%+ Multi-Year Service Contracts — Revenue You Can Count On

The Applied Global Services segment is a quiet superpower. Once a fab installs AMAT equipment, they sign long-term service contracts to keep it running. With 90%+ renewal rates on multi-year agreements, AMAT has a massive, predictable recurring revenue base that most pure-equipment companies lack. It also gives them privileged access to fab operations — insight that informs their next generation of tool design.

🟡 Risk

Semiconductor Cyclicality — The Industry Still Has Boom-Bust Patterns

Even with six consecutive growth years, the semiconductor equipment industry is inherently cyclical. A memory glut, a slowdown in AI capex, or a macro recession could compress WFE spending quickly. AMAT's AGS recurring revenue provides a buffer, but a significant chip downturn would impact the 73% of revenue that comes from new tool sales.

⚔️ Competitive Landscape

🔵 ASML

The lithography monopoly — makes the EUV machines that print chip patterns. Complementary to AMAT, not competitive. ASML does lithography; AMAT does everything around it. Together they're the two most critical equipment suppliers in the world.

🟡 Lam Research & KLA

The closest direct competitors. Lam leads in etch and deposition in some segments; KLA owns process control and inspection. The three form an oligopoly of semiconductor equipment. All benefit from the same AI investment cycle — this isn't zero-sum.

🟢 AMAT's Position

Largest and most diversified of the three. Broadest product portfolio across deposition, etch, implant, metrology, and services. The "full stack" equipment provider — one relationship for more of the fab workflow than any competitor.
